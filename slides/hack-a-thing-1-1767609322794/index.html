Benefit/ B-Corp

 

Benefit Corporation:

  • authorized by the gov as a
    for-profit corporate entity
  • stakeholders: society, workers, community, environment
  • impact+profit as part of incorporation company charter

 

B-Corp Certification:

  • 3rd-party certification based on social and environmental performance
  • corporate structure not a factor
* as part of this - we're thinking that every company we "start" should be a benefit corp/ b-corp - NOT an LLC or C corp etc * when you file your corporate CHarter

Why B-Corp Matters

For you as founders:

  • Forces early intentionality — values baked into charter can't be easily abandoned under growth pressure
  • Recruitment edge — talent actively seeks mission-driven companies
  • Investor signal — growing pool of impact investors; filters out misaligned VCs

For your product:

  • Expands design thinking — "who else is affected?" leads to better products
  • Long-term trust — users skeptical of tech; certification is a credibility shortcut

The uncomfortable truth:

  • Traditional C-Corp structure legally prioritizes shareholder returns
  • Officers can be sued for prioritizing impact over profit
  • B-Corp gives legal cover to make values-based decisions

Example: Kickstarter became a PBC in 2015 specifically so they could turn down acquisition offers that didn't align with their mission

* This is why we want you thinking about this from day one * Traditional corporate structure creates legal pressure to maximize shareholder value * Benefit corp status gives you legal protection to make decisions based on values, not just profit * Kickstarter example: they explicitly did this so they could say no to acquisitions that would have changed their mission * Etsy is an interesting counterexample - went B-Corp, then faced activist investor pressure after IPO - shows the real tensions
